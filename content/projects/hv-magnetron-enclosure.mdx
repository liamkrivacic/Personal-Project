A magnetron needs a few kilovolts of DC to sustain the crossed-field electron emission that
makes microwave power in the first place. For AtomCraft's RF heating system that supply sits
around 4 kV, and at 4 kV the engineering brief quietly changes. It's no longer just "make the
voltage"; it's "make the voltage in a box that a tired student can open at 9 pm without dying."
That framing shaped every decision on this page, which covers the circuit design and simulation,
the physical packaging, and the safety framework that ties them together.

<Callout title="Design scope">
To be upfront, this is **simulation and design work**: LTspice circuit exploration, the Fusion
360 enclosure, and the safety procedure planning. It's the full design of the supply, not a
commissioned, validated build.
</Callout>

## Circuit design

The goal was roughly 4 kV DC starting from a standard mains input. I chose a voltage-multiplying
rectifier after the high-voltage transformer because it lets a roughly 2 kV transformer secondary
reach the target DC voltage, instead of needing the transformer alone to supply the full 4 kV.
On a student project that gets carried around a lab, the weight and cost of the transformer is a
real consideration, not an afterthought.

### The voltage doubler

I started by modelling the post-transformer side of the supply as a 2 kV peak AC source in
LTspice. In other words, the schematic begins after the step-up transformer; the mains input and
transformer are upstream of this simulation. The first circuit was a half-wave voltage doubler:
on one half-cycle the capacitor charges from the transformer secondary, and on the opposite
half-cycle the stored capacitor voltage stacks with the source, pushing the output toward roughly
twice the 2 kV peak before load, diode drop, and ripple are accounted for.

Before I committed to any component values, I modelled the charge and discharge behaviour in
LTspice, since it's much cheaper to be wrong in a simulator than with a charged capacitor bank in
front of you:

### Full-wave voltage doubler

The half-wave doubler works, but its ripple is heavier than I wanted and it draws current in
lumps. So the second circuit I modelled was a full-wave voltage doubler. It still starts from the
same 2 kV peak transformer-secondary model, but now two diodes conduct on alternate half-cycles,
each charging its own capacitor. The output is taken from the stacked capacitor voltage, which
gives a more continuous charge path than the half-wave circuit. The simulation showed exactly
what I was after: lower ripple and a faster charge than the half-wave case at the same filter
capacitance:

The thing that kept nagging at me through all of this wasn't the voltage itself, it was the
energy parked in the capacitor bank. At 4 kV those capacitors hold enough stored energy to kill
someone long after the mains has been switched off and the supply "looks" dead. That single fact
is the reason the safety framework later treats capacitor discharge as a verified, enforced step
rather than a polite reminder.

## RF control circuit

Running a 1 kW magnetron out of a university lab means the operator can't be allowed to talk to
the high-voltage supply directly: there has to be something in between that refuses to do
anything dangerous. I led the research that set the architecture for that interlock chain, built
around an **ESP32-WROOM-32E** as the central commander: it takes operator commands over USB
serial, checks the interlock state, and only asserts the high-voltage enable when every condition
is satisfied. The human asks; the ESP32 decides. A teammate then designed the control-system PCB
from that research.

The rule the design couldn't break is that there's no conductive path between the low-voltage
control side and the 4 kV rail, anywhere. Every signal that crosses between the two worlds goes
through **optical isolation rated to 5000 V**: each interlock input and the enable output passes
through an optocoupler, so a fault on the high-voltage side physically cannot climb back into the
ESP32 or the operator's laptop on the other end of the USB cable.

For the actual switching the architecture uses **solid-state relays** instead of mechanical
contactors. No moving parts, no contact bounce, and crucially, no spark. A mechanical contactor
slamming shut on a capacitively-loaded high-voltage rail is an arc waiting to happen, and that's
the last thing you want inside this box.

### I/O summary

| Signal | Direction | Isolation | Function |
|--------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| HV Enable | Output | 5000 V optocoupler | Arms the solid-state relay to energise the supply |
| Interlock 1 | Input | 5000 V optocoupler | Cabinet door closed |
| Interlock 2 | Input | 5000 V optocoupler | Capacitor discharge confirmed |
| Interlock 3 | Input | 5000 V optocoupler | Over-voltage trip cleared |
| Temperature | Input | Isolated ADC | Transformer thermal monitor |
| Serial | I/O | USB (galvanic) | Operator command interface |

Underneath, the ESP32 runs a small state machine with one stubborn rule: HV Enable is asserted
only while all four interlock inputs are active at once, and the instant any one of them drops the
enable trips immediately, no matter what the operator just typed. The command is a request,
never an override.

## Enclosure design in Fusion 360

A circuit only becomes a real instrument once it lives in something you can build, cool and open
up again. I designed the enclosure in Fusion 360 as a stainless-steel sheet-metal part, complete
with the flat/net pattern for fabrication, ventilation cutouts sized for the transformer and
capacitor heat load, a power-entry cutout, and removable access panels for inspection and
maintenance.

Where things sit inside was a deliberate choice, not a packing exercise: transformer at the base
so the centre of mass is low and the unit is stable, capacitor bank kept away from the
access-panel side so you're not reaching past stored energy to do routine work, and cabling
routed clear of hot surfaces and sharp edges. I drew up a parts list alongside it so the thing
could actually be procured and built, not just admired in CAD.

## Safety framework

By the time I got here, "safety" wasn't a section I bolted on at the end, it was the thing that
had been quietly steering the circuit and the enclosure all along. Written out, the full framework
for the supply covers:

- **Mains isolation**: a lockable isolator on the incoming supply, so the cabinet cannot be
  energised while the access panel is open.
- **Capacitor discharge**: a bleed resistor across the capacitor bank and a mandatory
  discharge-and-verify step before any internal access. This step is enforced by Interlock 2
  in the control circuit: the HV Enable cannot be re-asserted until the discharge is
  confirmed.
- **Over-voltage protection**: a crowbar circuit monitors the DC rail and trips the supply
  if the voltage exceeds the safe operating limit. The trip clears Interlock 3 until
  manually reset.
- **Procedural controls**: short-pulse operation protocols to limit the energy in the
  transmission line during experiments; safe re-entry procedures that require two-person
  confirmation before restoring power after an interlock trip.
- **Physical barriers**: the enclosure isolates all high-voltage conductors from the
  operator surface; no bare conductors are within reach of a standard access panel opening.

The idea I kept coming back to is that you can't rely on people being careful when they're tired, 
you have to make the safe path the *easy* path. So the enclosure is laid out so that following
the procedure is genuinely the lowest-effort way to do anything: every step an operator takes to
get the cabinet open naturally routes them through a safe state first. Doing it the dangerous way
is actually more work.
