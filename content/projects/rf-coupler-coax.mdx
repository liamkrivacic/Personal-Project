I didn't set out to design a directional coupler. I set out to answer a much more annoying
question: how do you measure a kilowatt of microwave power flowing toward a plasma, and how
much of it is bouncing back, when you don't have a calibrated slotted line or a lab-grade
network analyser sitting on the bench? The coupler and the waveguide-to-coax transition on this
page are what I ended up building, but they're the *answer*, not the starting point. The
starting point was a pile of papers and a measurement I couldn't yet make.

AtomCraft's line carries roughly **1 kW at 2.45 GHz** in **WR340 waveguide** from a magnetron
toward a plasma, and two numbers end up driving the whole machine:

- **Forward power** tells us how much energy is actually reaching the load, and lets us compare
  the several waveguide builds we are manufacturing to find which has the lowest insertion loss.
- **Reflected power** tells us how badly the line is mismatched to the plasma. Minimising it with
  the three-stub tuner is how we impedance-match the load, keep the [standing-wave
  ratio](/projects/stub-tuner-hfss) low, and protect the magnetron from power reflected back into it.

Get those two numbers and the rest of the RF system has something to act on. That was the whole
job, and it's why I treated this as a *system* problem from the start rather than a
component-of-the-week.

<Callout title="Scope of this page">
This walks through the project in the order I actually did it: **preliminary research** to pick
an architecture, then the **detailed HFSS design** of the directional coupler and the
waveguide-to-coax transition, and finally the **power measurement PCB** that reads it all out.
Every performance figure here is a simulation result unless I say otherwise.
</Callout>

## Preliminary research: finding a buildable architecture

I spent the first stretch of this project just reading: PhD theses from tokamak
electron-cyclotron resonance heating (ECRH) programs, ion-thruster work, microwave-plasma
deposition rigs, anything that measured RF power in a plasma system. It was a slightly
demoralising survey, because almost every method I found quietly assumed instrumentation a
student team simply doesn't have: calibrated waveguide slotted lines, precision network
analysers, commercial in-line power meters. The physics was always thorough; what was missing
was how you actually *integrate* the measurement into a working diagnostic on a real budget.

The paper that turned it around was a 2024 one by Ferrand, Desangles and Elias: [**Non-intrusive
measurements of plasma impedance in an electron-cyclotron resonance thruster**](https://doi.org/10.1063/5.0191658).
Their method was genuinely buildable. A **dual directional coupler** taps a known fraction of
the incident and reflected signals, and the VNA recovers the load's full complex reflection
coefficient from the ratio $b_{\text{REF}}/b_{\text{FWD}}$, without ever interrupting the RF
system. That was the moment the project stopped being a reading list and became a design. It
became the template for AtomCraft's diagnostic.

Working from that reference, the diagnostic resolves into three components:

1. **A directional coupler** that peels off a small known fraction of the power (~40 dB down
   from 1 kW) and separates forward and reflected waves into different ports.
2. **A waveguide-to-coax transition** that gets the sampled power out of the WR340 waveguide
   and onto standard 50 Ω coaxial cable so it can reach the detector.
3. **A power measurement PCB** that rectifies the 2.45 GHz coaxial signal into a DC voltage
   proportional to power using a logarithmic detector IC, read by a Raspberry Pi.

### The design journey

The architecture did not arrive fully formed. It took four revisions, and the most useful one
came out of getting stuck.

- **Setup 1** was the obvious one: a coupler, a coax transition and a tuner in series. It
  immediately hit a wall I hadn't seen coming: one line can't simultaneously match the plasma
  *and* the measurement probe. Every time the three-stub tuner moved to track the plasma, it
  de-tuned my measurement probe and changed the effective coupling factor. The thing I was
  measuring with kept moving every time I tried to use it.
- **Setups 2–3** were me working the problem from the other side. Instead of chasing a perfect
  match I'll never hold, I redefined the **coupling factor** as the ratio of waveguide power to
  the power actually delivered to the detector, folding every mismatch into a single constant I
  could just measure once. Put **two identical coupler-to-coax stages in series**, read both, and
  that constant solves out directly. The dedicated measurement tuner I'd been agonising over
  disappeared entirely.
- **Setup 4** carried that idea into HFSS, where I could simulate coupling and directivity across
  the magnetron's emission bandwidth before committing anything to metal.

That progression (define the measurement, survey what's actually buildable, get stuck on the
integration, then design my way around it) is what turned a pile of papers into a concrete
component spec. The detour through Setup 1 wasn't wasted; it's what told me what the real
constraint was.

## Directional coupler

A directional coupler is a four-port device that samples a fixed fraction of main-line power
into a secondary port. The property I actually cared about is **directivity**: because of how
the coupling apertures combine in phase, forward and reflected waves come out of *different*
ports. That's what lets me read reflected power on its own, which is exactly the signal the
three-stub tuner needs to drive itself toward minimum reflection.

At WR340 and kilowatt power my options were narrow. Stripline and ferrite designs would simply
be destroyed at these power levels, so the only practical class is a **waveguide-aperture
coupler** machined straight into the guide wall. I evaluated three geometries:

<Figure
  src="/projects/rf-coupler-coax/coupler-types.png"
  alt="CAD drawings of three waveguide directional coupler types: cross-guide, broadwall, and loop"
  caption="Three coupler geometries evaluated for WR340 at 2.45 GHz: cross-guide (Moreno), broadwall, and loop coupler."
  width={714}

/>

Paul Wade's (W1GHZ) work on CNC-machined cross-guide couplers gave me a starting geometry I
could trust, his three-hole round-aperture design hits 35+ dB directivity in WR-42 and WR-19
using nothing but a CNC mill, which is precisely the fabrication path AtomCraft has access to.
The **Moreno cross-guide coupler** puts two X-shaped (or three round) apertures in the common
wall between two perpendicularly-crossed waveguides, spaced λg/4 apart so the coupled signals
add in the forward direction and cancel in the reverse:

<Figure
  src="/projects/rf-coupler-coax/moreno-schematic.png"
  alt="Moreno cross-guide coupler schematic showing X-shaped apertures, isolation port, output port, and coupling port at 45 degrees"

### HFSS simulation

HFSS (High Frequency Structure Simulator) solves Maxwell's equations with the finite element
method across a 3D model of the coupler, giving me S-parameters at every frequency across the
band. That let me numerically sweep the geometry (aperture cross-width, arm length, aperture
separation, probe depth) and hunt for a shape that meets the targets without cutting any
metal first. What I was chasing: flat −40 dB coupling with more than 25 dB directivity at
2.45 GHz, and crucially, a design that *stays* there across realistic machining tolerances.

I didn't try values by hand for long. The parameter space is too coupled (move one dimension
and three others shift), so I drove it with a sequence of search-based and gradient-based
optimisers instead. The sweep below is representative of what that process was up against:
each variable drags the S-parameter response around, which is exactly why a numerical search,
not intuition, was the right tool here:

<Figure

  alt="HFSS S-parameter plot showing a parametric sweep with S(in,in) resonance consistently below 2.45 GHz"
  caption="Representative parametric sweep from the HFSS optimisation process. The resonance tracks the swept parameter but sits below 2.45 GHz, illustrating the kind of result the optimiser had to navigate away from to find the final geometry."
  width={1667}
  height={870}

The optimiser converged on a geometry with coupling flat at −40 dB and directivity above
25 dB across the full band. But the result that actually changed my mind was a quieter one:
some of the best-performing parameter combinations sat on a knife-edge optimum, beautiful in
simulation, impossible to hold through machining. A coupler that's only perfect at exactly the
nominal dimensions is useless if the shop floor can't hit those dimensions. That realisation is
what pushed me toward the Monte Carlo tolerance study, and toward a fundamentally more forgiving
geometry.

So I moved to the **Moreno X-aperture geometry**, which trades a little peak performance for a
flatter, far more robust response across the manufacturing tolerance band. The Moreno design in

### Tolerance analysis

A Monte Carlo study randomised the key machining dimensions (hole size, wall thickness,
aperture placement) within realistic CNC tolerances and re-ran the simulation 60 times. The

Coupling stays tightly controlled (±0.5 dB) because it depends mostly on hole area, which is
easy to machine accurately. Isolation is the nervous one: it's sensitive to hole placement and
wall thickness, and it spans a 25 dB range across trials. The number I cared about was the
worst case, and the worst case still clears 25 dB directivity, which is enough to cleanly
separate forward from reverse power at the level this diagnostic needs. That's the difference
between a design that works on paper and one I'd actually hand to a machinist.

### VNA characterisation

A simulation is a prediction, not a result, and I wanted to be able to defend the −40 dB number
rather than just quote it. So the last stage of the design is a plan to measure the built coupler
against it. The catch: a directional coupler is a four-port device, and a standard VNA only sees
two ports at a time. The way around it is to terminate the unused ports in matched 50 Ω loads
and stitch together several two-port measurements until all four port combinations are covered.

#### De-embedding the test fixtures

The harder problem is that I'm not really measuring the coupler: I'm measuring the coupler plus
every cable and connector between it and the VNA, each adding its own phase shift and reflection.
I peel those away in two steps:

1. **SOLT calibration** (Short, Open, Load, Thru) shifts the VNA reference planes right up to the
   connector faces, removing the cable and adaptor effects entirely.
2. **T-parameter de-embedding** removes the test fixtures themselves.

The combined two-port measurement $M_{AB}$ contains the coupler sandwiched between two
test-fixture response matrices $T_A$ and $T_B$. I work in T-parameters rather than
S-parameters because they cascade by simple matrix multiplication:

$$
T_{\text{total}} = T_A \cdot T_{\text{DUT}} \cdot T_B
$$

For a symmetric fixture ($T_A = T_B$), the coupler's own response falls out as a matrix
square root, $M_T = \sqrt{M_{AB}}$, so I can recover the part I actually care about without
ever measuring the fixtures in isolation.

#### Target parameters

The four parameters that define the coupler's performance:

| Parameter | Physical meaning | Simulation target |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| S(3,1) | Coupling factor | −40 dB |
| S(4,1) | Isolation | < −65 dB |
| S(2,1) | Through-path insertion loss | < 0.1 dB |
| S(1,1) | Input return loss | < −25 dB |

Lining the measured values up against the HFSS predictions is what closes the loop on the whole
project. If they land inside the Monte Carlo tolerance bands, the simulation model is validated,
and just as importantly, I then know the coupling factor precisely enough to turn a detector
voltage straight back into main-line watts. That's the number the rest of the system has been
waiting for the entire time.

## Waveguide-to-coax transition

The sampled power leaves the coupler's coupled port still trapped in WR340 waveguide,
propagating as the **TE₁₀** mode, but the attenuator and detector IC downstream speak coax.
So I needed a transition that converts the waveguide TE₁₀ mode into the **TEM** mode that a
50 Ω coaxial cable carries.

The geometry I used is a **broad-wall probe**: the coax centre conductor reaches into the
waveguide through a Teflon insulator in the broad wall. A **back-short**, a short-circuit
termination sitting behind the probe, bounces the incident wave back so both contributions
hit the probe tip in phase. Tune the probe depth and the back-short distance together, and the
coax-port input impedance lands on 50 Ω at 2.45 GHz. It's a two-knob problem, which is exactly
the kind of thing HFSS is good at solving.

## Power measurement PCB

At the end of the chain, that coaxial signal still has to become a number a computer can read.
A Schottky diode rectifier would technically work, but its dynamic range is narrow and it
drifts with temperature, not what you want feeding a control loop. The part that fit was the
**AD8317 logarithmic detector IC**: it takes RF from 1 MHz to 10 GHz and puts out a DC voltage
that's linear in dBm across a 50 dB dynamic range, to ±1 dB, on about 150 mW.

The reason the log-linear part matters is downstream simplicity. Because $V_{\text{out}}$ is
proportional to $P_{\text{in}}$ in dBm, the Raspberry Pi can read the ADC and convert straight
to power with one linear equation, no lookup table, no calibration curve baked into firmware.
The cleverness in the analog part buys simplicity in the embedded part.

Ahead of the AD8317 sits a **15 dB inline attenuator** to drop the coupled power (around
−10 dBm at 1 kW main-line power with −40 dB coupling) into the IC's sweet spot. The **ADL5330**
variable attenuator was on the table as an active alternative, but for this job the fixed passive
part won on simplicity and predictable loss at 2.45 GHz. There was nothing the variable gain
was going to earn here.

### Circuit design

A team member turned the detector requirements into the schematic below. The
AD8317 runs from a 3.3 V supply (VPOS), with a 52.3 Ω input resistor and an 8 kΩ feedback
resistor setting the output slope and bypass capacitors setting the output bandwidth. SMA female
50 Ω connectors handle all the RF; VOUT feeds straight to the Raspberry Pi ADC, with an on-board
3.3 V regulator (VREG) for supply flexibility:
