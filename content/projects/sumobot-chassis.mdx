The UNSW SumoBots competition puts autonomous robots against each other in an arena with a
white-bordered ring. The objective is simple: push the opponent out without crossing the line
yourself. The advanced stream uses more capable hardware and gives the robots more room to
implement strategy.

I won the advanced stream in August 2024.

**Advanced stream winner**, UNSW SumoBots, August 2024. The robot ran fully autonomously —

## Mechanical design

The chassis was designed in Fusion 360 and manufactured using a combination of laser cutting
and 3D printing. Laser cutting produced the flat structural panels with precise slot-and-tab
joints; 3D printing handled the sensor mounts and motor brackets where geometry was too
complex for flat-sheet work.

The chassis geometry was iterated across multiple revisions. Key decisions included keeping
the centre of mass low (reduces tipping when pushing), keeping the footprint compact (harder
to get under), and positioning the sensors so they have clear sightlines without extending
past the robot's profile where they could be targeted.

## Sensors and detection

Three ultrasonic distance sensors cover forward, front-left, and front-right arcs. This
gives the robot enough information to detect an opponent in front of it and bias its turn
toward the strongest return — useful for homing in on a partially-visible target rather than
committing to a blind forward charge.

IR line sensors under the chassis detect the white boundary line. When a line is detected
the robot interrupts its current behaviour and executes an escape manoeuvre away from the
edge before resuming the search pattern.

## Control logic

The Arduino firmware coordinates the two subsystems: opponent detection and line avoidance.
The main loop structure is:

1. Check IR sensors first — line avoidance takes priority over any other action.
2. If no line is detected, read the three ultrasonic sensors.
3. If an opponent is within range, drive forward or bias toward the strongest return.
4. If no opponent is detected, execute a slow rotation search pattern.

Motor control uses PWM to set drive speed independently on each side. The start delay
(a fixed pause after power-on) gives the human referee time to clear the arena before the
robot becomes active — a competition requirement.

## What the build demonstrated

SumoBots is primarily a systems integration challenge: mechanical, electronic, and software
decisions interact in ways that are hard to reason about separately. The chassis geometry
affects sensor mounting positions; sensor positions affect the software logic; software
aggressiveness affects how the chassis performs under impact. Getting all of these to work
together in a competition setting — where you get one chance and cannot adjust between rounds
— is a useful constraint that mirrors real embedded systems work.

Winning the advanced stream was the result of iterating on all three layers together, not
optimising each one independently.
