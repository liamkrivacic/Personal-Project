A plasma load won't hold still. Its impedance drifts as the operating conditions change, and a
fixed-tuned waveguide circuit has no way to follow it: reflected power climbs, efficiency
falls, and the magnetron starts taking a beating. A three-stub tuner fixes that by adjusting the
line impedance on the fly, but it comes with its own problem: there are three stub depths to set,
and finding the right combination by hand means searching a three-dimensional space one slow
simulation at a time. Under live conditions that's a non-starter.

So I stopped tuning it by hand and made HFSS do it. This project wraps an IronPython script
around ANSYS HFSS that turns the simulator into a gradient-descent loop (perturb the stubs,
watch the reflected power, step downhill, repeat) running completely unattended against a
simulated plasma load.

<Callout title="Key result (simulated)">
In one test case the script pulled the load reflection coefficient from roughly **0.88 down to
0.25** across **20 automated tuning steps**, with no human in the loop. These are HFSS simulation
results, not measurements on a live system.
</Callout>

## Choosing an approach

Before writing any code I had to decide *what* the loop would actually measure, and I went back
and forth between two options.

**Method 1: phase-based direct computation.** An AD8302 gain/phase detector reads both the
magnitude *and* phase of the reflected signal relative to the incident wave. With the full
complex reflection coefficient in hand at every step, you can in principle just *solve* for the
stub positions from the impedance-transformation equations: no searching, no iteration.

**Method 2: gradient search on reflected power only.** A log power detector reads one scalar:
reflected power. You nudge the stubs, watch which way the power moves, and step in the direction
that makes it smaller. No phase measurement anywhere.

I went with the gradient search. Method 1 is elegant on paper, but it leans on accurate phase
data, and accurate phase means a known reference plane right at the plasma face plus careful
AD8302 calibration, exactly the kind of thing that's fragile when you eventually move from
simulation to a real, slightly-wrong installation. The gradient approach only ever needs scalar
power, so it's simpler to instrument and far more forgiving of cable-length and reference errors.
The price is more simulation steps per tuning cycle, and for an automated loop I was happy to
pay it in compute rather than in calibration fragility.

## Why a three-stub tuner

The three-stub tuner is the standard choice for high-power WR340 because it shrugs off the
kilowatt power levels that would vaporise a thin-film or ferrite matching network. Each stub is
just a short-circuit plunger pushed into the broad wall of the waveguide; how far it goes in sets
the susceptance at that point on the line. Space three of them a quarter-wavelength apart and the
tuner can reach any load impedance inside its range.

It also fit my constraints. A four-probe reflectometer with full phase measurement would have
needed more instrumentation than AtomCraft had at the design stage, whereas the three-stub
gradient approach asks for almost nothing: perturb the stubs, read the reflected power, step
toward the minimum. The hardware stays cheap and the intelligence lives in the loop.

## The gradient descent algorithm

The IronPython script reaches into HFSS through its COM scripting interface, setting stub depths
and reading reflected-power results back out with nobody touching the mouse. Stripped to its
bones, the loop is:

1. Simulate the current stub configuration and record reflected power.
2. Perturb each stub depth by a small step and re-simulate.
3. Estimate the gradient (change in reflected power per unit stub movement).
4. Move all three stubs in the direction of lower reflected power.
5. Repeat until the reflection coefficient falls below a target threshold or the step
   count is reached.

The tuning constant sets how aggressively each step moves, and the most useful thing the
simulation runs taught me is that overshoot is real. Crank the tuning constant up and the
algorithm flies past the minimum and starts oscillating around it instead of settling. It's the
same failure mode anyone who's set a learning rate too high will recognise, except here it's stub
plungers hunting back and forth instead of a loss curve. That's a real constraint, not a
footnote: before this loop ever runs on live hardware, the tuning constant has to be calibrated
to the sensitivity of each load profile, or it'll chase its own tail.

## The reflected power landscape

Before turning the optimiser loose on the full three-dimensional search, I wanted to understand
the terrain it would be walking over. The clearest picture of it isn't mine: it's from
Chtcherbakov and Swart's 1997 automatic three-stub tuner for plasma deposition, which drives the
stubs by steepest descent on reflected power alone, the same method my script uses. Their measured
contour map, with real minimization tracks drawn over it, is almost exactly the landscape my own
gradient has to cross:

That single basin is what makes a gradient approach viable. Because the reflected-power surface
has one minimum rather than many, steepest descent funnels into the true match instead of stalling
in a local pocket, which is exactly the behaviour those four tracks show. If the landscape had been
lumpy with several minima, the whole strategy would have needed rethinking, so confirming the shape
was a genuine go/no-go check before I committed to building my own loop in HFSS.

## Convergence on the Smith chart

My favourite way to watch the algorithm work is on a Smith chart, where the load reflection
coefficient traces a path through the normalised impedance plane as the loop iterates. Starting
from a badly mismatched load way out at the edge, every tuning step nudges the impedance closer
to the centre, the point where $|\Gamma| = 0$ and the match is perfect:

A clean inward spiral like this one means the step size and tuning constant were well-matched to
the load. Run the same load with a higher tuning constant and you get the opposite picture: the trajectory
orbits the centre and oscillates instead of landing on it, which is the overshoot failure made
visible. Seeing both behaviours side by side is what convinced me the calibration
step isn't optional.

The bigger takeaway for me was that IronPython can turn HFSS from a single-shot solver into an
active search loop that improves its own design. That's the foundation any future automated
matching workflow in the AtomCraft RF system would be built on.

## Source code

<CodeBlock title="gradient-descent-v1.py - IronPython script for ANSYS HFSS">

```python
### Required setup ###
import ScriptEnv
ScriptEnv.Initialize("Ansoft.ElectronicsDesktop")
oDesktop.RestoreWindow()
import os

## Get S11 (dB) at the frequency closest to target_freq_hz from an HFSS design.
## mode=1 returns the S11 value; mode=0 returns the closest frequency.
def get_s11_or_frequency(oDesign, sweep_name, target_freq_hz, mode):
    oModule = oDesign.GetModule("ReportSetup")
    oResults = oModule.GetSolutionDataPerVariation(
        "Terminal Solution Data",
        sweep_name,
        ["Domain:=", "Sweep"],
        ["Freq:=", ["All"]],
        ["dB(St(1,1))"]
    )
    NominalData = oResults[0]
    SweepValues = NominalData.GetSweepValues("Freq", True)
    DataValues = NominalData.GetRealDataValues("dB(St(1,1))")
    closest_index = min(range(len(SweepValues)), key=lambda i: abs(SweepValues[i] - target_freq_hz))
    if mode == 1:
        return DataValues[closest_index]
    else:
        return SweepValues[closest_index]

## Set a local variable in the HFSS design (appends "mm" to numeric values).
def change_local_variable(oDesign, var_name, var_value):
    var_value_str = str(var_value) + "mm" if isinstance(var_value, (int, float)) else str(var_value)
    oDesign.ChangeProperty([
        "NAME:AllTabs",
        [
            "NAME:LocalVariableTab",
            ["NAME:PropServers", "LocalVariables"],
            ["NAME:ChangedProps", ["NAME:" + var_name, "Value:=", var_value_str]]
        ]
    ])

def create_report(oDesign):
    freq = get_s11_or_frequency(oDesign, "Setup1 : Sweep", TARGET_FREQ_HZ, 0)
    oModule = oDesign.GetModule("ReportSetup")
    oModule.CreateReport(
        "Tuning Chart", "Modal Solution Data", "Smith Chart", "Setup1 : Sweep", [],
        ["Freq:=", [freq],
         "a:=", ["Nominal"], "b:=", ["Nominal"], "l:=", ["Nominal"],
         "w:=", ["Nominal"], "f:=", ["Nominal"], "s:=", ["Nominal"],
         "d:=", ["Nominal"], "d1:=", ["All"], "d2:=", ["All"], "d3:=", ["All"]],
        ["Polar Component:=", ["S(1,1)"]]
    )

def update_report(oDesign):
    freq = get_s11_or_frequency(oDesign, "Setup1 : Sweep", TARGET_FREQ_HZ, 0)
    oModule = oDesign.GetModule("ReportSetup")
    oModule.UpdateTraces(
        "Tuning Chart", ["S(1,1)"], "Setup1 : Sweep", [],
        ["Freq:=", [freq],
         "a:=", ["Nominal"], "b:=", ["Nominal"], "l:=", ["Nominal"],
         "w:=", ["Nominal"], "f:=", ["Nominal"], "s:=", ["Nominal"],
         "d:=", ["Nominal"], "d1:=", ["All"], "d2:=", ["All"], "d3:=", ["All"]],
        ["Polar Component:=", ["S(1,1)"]]
    )

### Project setup ###
oProject = oDesktop.GetActiveProject()
oDesign = oProject.GetActiveDesign()
oModule = oDesign.GetModule("Solutions")

TARGET_FREQ_HZ = 2.45e9
SWEEP_NAME = "Setup2 : Sweep"
ITERATIONS = 20
SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE = 0.5   # mm: perturbation step for gradient estimation
STEP_SIZE_CONST = 3           # tuning aggressiveness; reduce if oscillation occurs

### Initialise all stubs at 0 mm insertion ###
d1_length = d2_length = d3_length = 0.0
change_local_variable(oDesign, "d1", d1_length)
change_local_variable(oDesign, "d2", d2_length)
change_local_variable(oDesign, "d3", d3_length)

### Gradient descent loop ###
for x in range(ITERATIONS):
    results_mag = []

    # Baseline S11 at current stub positions
    oDesign.Analyze("Setup1 : Sweep")
    results_mag.append(get_s11_or_frequency(oDesign, "Setup1 : Sweep", TARGET_FREQ_HZ, 1))

    if x == 0:
        create_report(oDesign)
    else:
        update_report(oDesign)

    # Perturb each stub in turn and record S11
    for var, length in [("d1", d1_length), ("d2", d2_length), ("d3", d3_length)]:
        new_length = max(0, min(43.2, length + SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE))
        change_local_variable(oDesign, var, new_length)
        oDesign.Analyze(SWEEP_NAME)
        results_mag.append(get_s11_or_frequency(oDesign, SWEEP_NAME, TARGET_FREQ_HZ, 1))

    # Estimate gradient (dS11/d_stub) for each stub
    results_grad = [
        (results_mag[1] - results_mag[0]) / SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE,
        (results_mag[2] - results_mag[1]) / SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE,
        (results_mag[3] - results_mag[2]) / SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE,
    ]

    # Normalised gradient step
    grad_magnitude = sum(g ** 2 for g in results_grad) ** 0.5
    step_size = STEP_SIZE_CONST / grad_magnitude

    # Move stubs in direction of decreasing S11
    d1_length = max(0, min(43.2, d1_length - SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE - step_size * results_grad[0]))
    d2_length = max(0, min(43.2, d2_length - SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE - step_size * results_grad[1]))
    d3_length = max(0, min(43.2, d3_length - SMALL_POSITION_CHANGE - step_size * results_grad[2]))

    change_local_variable(oDesign, "d1", d1_length)
    change_local_variable(oDesign, "d2", d2_length)
    change_local_variable(oDesign, "d3", d3_length)
```
