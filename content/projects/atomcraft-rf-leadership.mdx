AtomCraft is a student engineering project targeting kilowatt-level injected microwave power
into a plasma test vessel. The RF system — the chain of components between the magnetron and
the vessel — is the highest-risk and highest-complexity subsystem in the project. My role
was RF Engineer and RF Team Lead, responsible for the technical design of that chain and for
coordinating the team that built it.

<Callout title="Team scope">
Led a **9-person cross-disciplinary RF team** across simulation, power monitoring, impedance
matching, safety compliance, and experiment planning. The sources support design and
coordination work; final commissioning evidence is not available.
</Callout>

## The RF transmission line

The system runs at 2.45 GHz in WR340 waveguide — the same ISM-band frequency used in
industrial microwave heating. The transmission line connects the magnetron to the plasma
vessel through a chain of functional components:

**Magnetron → waveguide adaptor → isolator / dummy load → directional coupler → three-stub
tuner → RF port window → vessel**

Each component in this chain has a specific function. The isolator protects the magnetron
from reflected power when the plasma load is mismatched. The directional coupler samples
forward and reflected power for monitoring. The three-stub tuner adjusts the line impedance
to reduce reflections as the plasma load changes. The RF port window seals the vacuum vessel
while passing microwave power into the plasma.

## My role across the chain

Design ownership meant understanding every component deeply enough to review others' work
and catch errors before they become hardware problems. In practice that meant:

- Leading the HFSS simulation work for the directional coupler, waveguide-to-coax transition,
  and three-stub tuner (see the individual project pages for detail on each).
- Reviewing subsystem designs from other team members and coordinating integration between
  electrical, mechanical, and vacuum engineering subgroups.
- Writing and reviewing safety compliance documentation for high-power microwave operation —
  exposure limits, interlock requirements, personnel protection procedures, and experiment
  protocols.
- Running weekly team updates, splitting work into packages, and managing recruitment and
  onboarding as the team grew.

## Safety and compliance

High-power microwave work at 2.45 GHz requires careful management of RF exposure risk.
The compliance documentation covered the applicable exposure standards, the physical
barriers and interlocks required at each power level, the procedures for safe operation
and re-entry after a fault, and the responsibilities of each person in the experimental
team.

Writing this material was not bureaucratic compliance work; it was a design input. The
safety requirements constrained where components could be placed, which access panels
needed interlocking, and how the experiment startup sequence had to be ordered.

## What the role demonstrated

Technical leadership on a complex engineering project is different from individual
contribution. The skill is in maintaining design coherence across subsystems and people:
ensuring that the decisions made in one area do not create problems in another, that
the work is distributed at the right level of decomposition, and that the team has
what it needs to make progress without constant intervention.

The AtomCraft RF system is the project where I learned those skills under real engineering
constraints — not in a controlled assignment environment but in a multi-year, multi-person
project with physical hardware, safety obligations, and an actual target to deliver.
