AtomCraft is a student engineering project building a kilowatt-level microwave heating system for
a plasma test vessel. The RF chain — everything between the magnetron and the vessel — is the
highest-risk part of the build, and it's what I was responsible for. As RF Engineer and RF Team
Lead I owned the technical design and led the nine-person team that executed it.

<Callout title="Team scope">
Led a **9-person cross-disciplinary RF team** across simulation, power monitoring, impedance
matching, safety compliance, and experiment planning. The system is moving toward final
commissioning.
</Callout>

## The transmission line

The system runs at 2.45 GHz in WR340 waveguide. The line connects the magnetron to the plasma
vessel through:

**Magnetron → waveguide adaptor → isolator / dummy load → directional coupler → three-stub
tuner → RF port window → vessel**

Each component earns its place: the isolator shields the magnetron from reflected power, the
coupler samples forward and reflected power, the stub tuner re-matches the line as the plasma
load drifts, and the RF window seals the vacuum vessel while passing the microwave power through.

## What I did

- Led HFSS simulation work for the directional coupler, waveguide-to-coax transition, and
  three-stub tuner (see the individual project pages).
- Reviewed subsystem designs from team members and coordinated integration across electrical,
  mechanical, and vacuum engineering subgroups.
- Wrote safety compliance documentation: exposure limits, interlock requirements, personnel
  protection procedures, and experiment protocols.
- Ran weekly team updates, split work into packages, and managed recruitment and onboarding as the
  team grew.

## Safety

High-power 2.45 GHz work carries a real RF exposure risk. Writing the compliance docs forced me
to treat safety as a design input, not an afterthought: the requirements decided where components
could go, which panels had to be interlocked, and what the experiment startup sequence had to be.

## What I took away

Leading a technical team is a different skill from being a strong individual contributor.
The hard part is keeping the design coherent across subsystems and across people: catching a
decision in the electrical group before it breaks something in the vacuum group, sizing work
packages right, and giving people enough context to run without me over their shoulder. AtomCraft
is where I learned that under real constraints — multi-year, multi-person, real hardware, real
safety obligations.
