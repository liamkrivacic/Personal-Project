AtomCraft is a student engineering project chasing something genuinely hard: injecting
kilowatt-level microwave power into a plasma test vessel. The RF system, the whole chain of
components between the magnetron and the vessel, is the highest-risk, highest-complexity part of
that, and it's the part I was responsible for. As RF Engineer and RF Team Lead I owned the
technical design of that chain and led the team that built it, which meant I was on the hook both
for the physics being right and for nine other people being able to make progress.

<Callout title="Team scope">
Led a **9-person cross-disciplinary RF team** across simulation, power monitoring, impedance
matching, safety compliance, and experiment planning. This page is about the design and
coordination work; the system is still moving toward final commissioning.
</Callout>

## The RF transmission line

The system runs at 2.45 GHz in WR340 waveguide, the same ISM-band frequency used in
industrial microwave heating. The transmission line connects the magnetron to the plasma
vessel through a chain of functional components:

**Magnetron → waveguide adaptor → isolator / dummy load → directional coupler → three-stub
tuner → RF port window → vessel**

Every component in that chain earns its place. The isolator shields the magnetron from reflected
power when the plasma load is mismatched. The directional coupler samples forward and reflected
power so we can actually see what the line is doing. The three-stub tuner re-matches the line as
the plasma load drifts. And the RF port window seals the vacuum vessel while still letting the
microwave power through. Part of leading the team was being able to hold all of these in my head
at once and see how a change in one rippled into the next.

## My role across the chain

For me, owning the design meant understanding every component well enough to review someone
else's work on it and catch the mistake while it was still a line on a drawing, not a ruined
piece of aluminium. In practice that looked like:

- Leading the HFSS simulation work for the directional coupler, waveguide-to-coax transition,
  and three-stub tuner (see the individual project pages for detail on each).
- Reviewing subsystem designs from other team members and coordinating integration between
  electrical, mechanical, and vacuum engineering subgroups.
- Writing and reviewing safety compliance documentation for high-power microwave operation:
  exposure limits, interlock requirements, personnel protection procedures, and experiment
  protocols.
- Running weekly team updates, splitting work into packages, and managing recruitment and
  onboarding as the team grew.

## Safety and compliance

High-power microwave work at 2.45 GHz comes with a real RF exposure risk, and managing it was on
me. I wrote compliance documentation covering the exposure standards that applied, the physical
barriers and interlocks needed at each power level, the procedures for operating safely and for
re-entering the space after a fault, and who on the team was responsible for what.

I came to see this as some of the most genuinely useful work I did, not box-ticking. The safety
requirements were a design input: they decided where components could physically go, which
access panels had to be interlocked, and what order the experiment startup sequence had to run in.
Writing the rules forced clarity on the design.

## What the role demonstrated

What I took away is that leading a technical project is a different skill from being a strong
individual contributor, and I had to learn it the real way. The hard part isn't any one
calculation; it's keeping the design coherent across subsystems *and* across people: making sure
a decision in the electrical group doesn't quietly break something in the vacuum group, breaking
work into pieces at the right size, and giving people enough context to run without me hovering
over them.

AtomCraft is where I learned that under actual constraints rather than in a tidy assignment: a
multi-year, multi-person project with real hardware, real safety obligations, and a real target
to hit. It's the experience I'd most want to be judged on.
